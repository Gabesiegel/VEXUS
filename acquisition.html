VExUS Image Acquisition Guide

Overview

Why Use VExUS?

  • Non-invasive Quantification: Quantify venous congestion without invasive procedures
  • Real-Time Hemodynamic Insight: Immediate information about patient's hemodynamic status
  • Guiding Therapy: Assess response to treatments like diuretics
  • Organ Dysfunction Link: Higher scores correlate with organ dysfunction

Indications for VExUS

  • Suspected volume overload
  • Heart Failure
  • Hemodynamic AKI
  • Shock states
  • Unexplained hypotension

Equipment and Preparation

  • Ultrasound Machine: 2D imaging, color Doppler, and pulsed-wave Doppler capabilities
  • Transducer: Phased array or curvilinear
  • ECG Module: Highly recommended
  • Supplies: Ultrasound gel and gloves
  • Patient Position: Supine, examination table at operator's waist level

1. Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) Assessment

IVC Assessment Technique

  • Short Axis: Place transducer 1-2 cm below xiphoid process, orientation marker at 3 o'clock
  • Long Axis: Rotate transducer 90° counterclockwise (marker to 12 o'clock)
  • Measurement: 2 cm below RA-IVC junction or 1 cm below HV-IVC junction

Pitfalls:

  • Distinguishing IVC from aorta
  • Dilated IVC in athletes
  • Collapsed IVC despite elevated RAP
  • "Cylinder effect" with long-axis measurements

2. Hepatic Vein Assessment

Hepatic Vein Doppler Assessment

Normal Waveform Components:

  • A Wave: Atrial contraction
  • S Wave: Ventricular systole
  • V Wave: Tricuspid annulus return
  • D Wave: Ventricular diastole

Pitfalls:

  • Atrial fibrillation (absent A wave)
  • Rhythm abnormalities without ECG
  • Liver cirrhosis effects
  • Significant tricuspid regurgitation

3. Portal Vein Assessment

Portal Vein Flow Analysis

PVPF = [(Vmax - Vmin) / Vmax] x 100%

  • Normal: PVPF < 30%
  • Mild congestion: PVPF 30-50%
  • Severe congestion: PVPF > 50%

Pitfalls:

  • Increased pulsatility in athletes
  • Cirrhosis effects
  • Respiratory variations confusion

4. Intrarenal Vein Assessment

Intrarenal Vein Doppler Assessment

Pre-Doppler Optimization:

  • Slide transducer caudally for kidney visualization
  • Use zoom function for renal parenchyma
  • Place color box over parenchyma

Interpretation:

  • Normal: Continuous flow
  • Mild-Moderate: Pulsatile with S and D waves
  • Severe: Monophasic (D-only)

Pitfalls:

  • Technical challenges with breath-holding
  • Limited studies in chronic kidney disease
  • Transplant recipient considerations

VExUS Grading System

Grade Criteria
Grade 0 IVC ≤ 2 cm
Grade 1 IVC > 2 cm, one or more mildly abnormal Doppler waveforms
Grade 2 IVC > 2 cm, one severely abnormal waveform
Grade 3 IVC > 2 cm, two or more severely abnormal waveforms

Important Caveats and Limitations

  • Does not differentiate between volume and pressure overload
  • Must be integrated with clinical context
  • Not a volume responsiveness tool
  • Limited study populations
  • Assesses only part of hemodynamic circuit
  • Should not be performed in isolation
