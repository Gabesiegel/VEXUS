VExUS Image Acquisition Guide

Overview

Why Use VExUS?

  • Non-invasive quantification of venous congestion
  • Real-time hemodynamic insight for immediate assessment
  • Guide therapeutic interventions and monitor treatment response
  • Early detection of organ dysfunction risk

Clinical Indications

  • Suspected volume overload
  • Heart Failure
  • Hemodynamic AKI
  • Shock states
  • Unexplained hypotension

Required Equipment

  • Ultrasound machine with 2D imaging, color Doppler, and pulsed-wave Doppler
  • Phased array or curvilinear transducer
  • ECG module (recommended)
  • Ultrasound gel and gloves

1. Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) Assessment

Technique

  • Place transducer 1-2 cm below xiphoid process
  • Measure maximal IVC diameter 2 cm below RA-IVC junction
  • Assess in both short and long axis views

Key Considerations

  • Differentiate from aorta
  • Account for athletic adaptation
  • Consider intra-abdominal pressure effects

2. Hepatic Vein Assessment

Waveform Analysis

  • A Wave: Atrial contraction
  • S Wave: Ventricular systole
  • V Wave: Tricuspid return
  • D Wave: Ventricular diastole

Important Considerations

  • Assess for atrial fibrillation effects
  • Consider liver disease impact
  • Account for tricuspid regurgitation

3. Portal Vein Assessment

PVPF Calculation

PVPF = [(Vmax - Vmin) / Vmax] x 100%

  • Normal: PVPF < 30%
  • Mild congestion: PVPF 30-50%
  • Severe congestion: PVPF > 50%

Technical Considerations

  • Account for respiratory variation
  • Consider cirrhosis effects
  • Optimize Doppler angle

4. Intrarenal Vein Assessment

Technique Optimization

  • Optimize renal parenchyma visualization
  • Use color Doppler to locate vessels
  • Assess interlobar vessel waveforms

Pattern Recognition

  • Normal: Continuous flow
  • Mild-Moderate: Biphasic (S/D waves)
  • Severe: Monophasic pattern

Important Limitations

  • Does not differentiate volume vs pressure overload
  • Must be integrated with clinical context

  • Assess only part of hemodynamic circuit
