VExUS Image Acquisition Guide

Overview

Why Use VExUS?

  • Non-invasive quantification of venous congestion
  • Real-time hemodynamic insight for immediate assessment
  • Guide therapeutic interventions and monitor treatment response
  • Early detection of organ dysfunction risk

Image Acquisition

Below are infographics depicting the recommended ultrasound views for each venous waveform assessment. Each view can be obtained using pulsed wave Doppler to capture the venous flow patterns crucial for calculating VExUS scores.

IVC Assessment

Key Points:

  • Place patient in supine position
  • Use a low frequency (curvilinear) probe
  • Obtain subxiphoid or subcostal view to visualize IVC
  • Measure IVC diameter 2-3 cm from right atrial junction
  • Assess for inspiratory collapse (if applicable)
  • Record maximum diameter for VExUS classification

Hepatic Vein Assessment

Key Points:

  • Locate hepatic vein using right intercostal view
  • Use color Doppler to identify vein
  • Place pulsed wave gate in the hepatic vein branch
  • Ensure gate is 1-2 cm away from IVC junction
  • Use low wall filter settings
  • Observe for triphasic normal waveform (S > D)

Normal Waveform:

Triphasic with dominant systolic (S) wave, smaller diastolic (D) wave, and small retrograde A wave during atrial contraction.

Abnormal Patterns:

  • Mild: S wave < D wave, preserved direction
  • Severe: Loss of phasicity, retrograde flow, or monophasic pattern

Portal Vein Assessment

Key Points:

  • Use intercostal or subxiphoid approach
  • Identify portal vein at liver hilum
  • Distinguished by echogenic walls and hepatic artery nearby
  • Use color Doppler to confirm flow direction
  • Place pulsed wave gate in main portal vein
  • Ensure low wall filter settings

Normal Waveform:

Continuous, mildly pulsatile antegrade flow toward the liver.

Abnormal Patterns:

  • Mild: Increased pulsatility but preserved antegrade flow
  • Severe: Bidirectional ("to-and-fro") flow or reversal of flow

Renal Vein Assessment

Key Points:

  • Use intercostal or flank approach to kidney
  • Identify renal vein at the renal hilum
  • Use color Doppler to confirm location
  • Place pulsed wave gate in the interlobar veins
  • For better accuracy, assess both kidneys if possible
  • Right kidney offers easier technical access

Normal Waveform:

Continuous, monophasic antegrade flow toward the IVC.

Abnormal Patterns:

  • Mild: Biphasic pattern with preserved antegrade flow
  • Severe: Pulsatile or discontinuous flow pattern

Tips and Troubleshooting

General Tips:

  • Use proper patient positioning (slight right side tilt can help visualize hepatic and portal veins)
  • Adjust Doppler angle to <60Â° for accurate velocity measurements
  • Use low wall filter settings to capture low-velocity venous flow
  • Hold the probe steady and ask patient to hold breath briefly for clearer images

Challenges and Solutions:

  • Poor acoustic window: Try different probe positions or patient positions
  • Respiratory variations: Time measurements with respiration or ask for breath hold
  • Arrhythmias: Take multiple measurements over several cardiac cycles
  • Technical artifacts: Adjust gain, depth, and focus settings
